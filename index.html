The Effectiveness of Various Anti-Aging Compounds in Model Organisms

In my Data Visualization course, I was able to analyze a dataset of my choice. Using R and RStudio, I explored a dataset and presented a story about the data to my classmates. My project analyzed the effectiveness of various anti-aging compounds in different model organisms.

I decided to utilize the DrugAge database provided by the The Human Ageing Genomic Resources (HAGR). The DrugAge database aims to provide "high‐quality summary data on lifespan‐extending drugs and compounds in model organisms" (Barardo et al., 2017). This database contains 324 research articles from the scientific community. From this data, statistically significant lifespan extension data can be examined.

Since the DrugAge database originally contains over 1000 entries, my goal was to cleanup and wrangle the data to focus on larger correlations. While the DrugAge database contains a variety of compounds that have been shown to increase the lifespan in different model organisms, which compounds should be prioritized and studied more to potentially combat human mortality? I also aimed to discover which compounds and dosages showed the most success in specific organisms.

Gene annotation uncovers the evolutionary significance of STUB1 in D. melanogaster

STUB1 is known to participate in protein degradation and regulation of the insulin signaling pathway. The purpose of this experiment was to annotate STUB1 in D. melanogaster, D. simulans, D. virilis, and D. willistoni as well as discover its evolutionary significance. To accomplish this, the GEP UCSC Genome Browser Gateway and NCBI Blast were utilized in the analysis and annotation of STUB1 in each species. This analysis showed that STUB1 is mostly conserved in these species and that the genomic neighborhood of STUB1 changed drastically across time. The conservation of STUB1 in each target species demonstrates that STUB1 is evolutionarily significant and is a critical player in the insulin pathway.
